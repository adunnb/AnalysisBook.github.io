**Axiom (Completeness of $\mathbb{R}$):** Every non-empty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above has a least upper bound (supremum) in $\mathbb{R}$.

This property is also called the **least upper bound property** of $\mathbb{R}$. It is what distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$ — the rational numbers are an ordered field but fail to satisfy completeness.

**Example:** Consider the set $A = \{q \in \mathbb{Q} : q^2 < 2\}$. This set is bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$ (for instance by $2$), but its supremum $\sqrt{2}$ does not exist in $\mathbb{Q}$. In $\mathbb{R}$, completeness guarantees that $\sup A = \sqrt{2} \in \mathbb{R}$.

**Remark:** The completeness axiom is not exactly a theorem. It is an axiom, meaning it is taken as a fundamental property that defines $\mathbb{R}$. All the major theorems of real analysis ultimately rest on this property.
